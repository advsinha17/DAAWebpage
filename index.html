Problem Statement

The goal of the assignment is to implement two algorithms for Densest Subgraph Discovery (DSD) in graph G. The densest subgraph of a graph is the subgraph with the highest edge density (number of edges over number of vertices). The algorithms proposed define density based on h-cliques, which is a complete subgraph of size h. The DSD algorithm then attempts to find a subgraph with the highest h-clique density.
The h-clique density of a graph is defined as the number of h-cliques in the graph, divided by the number of vertices in the graph. The algorithms we implement aim to find the subgraph of a given graph with the highest h-clique density. Both algorithms make use of binary search to solve a maximum flow problem for the same. A flow network is a directed graph with a source, a sink, and some intermediate nodes. Each edge has a capacity and the amount of flow on an edge cannot exceed the capacity of the edge. The maximum flow of a flow network equals the capacity of its minimum st-cut. The second algorithm improves on the first algorithm by using tighter bounds of the upper and lower bounds in the binary search.

Applications of Densest Subgraph Discovery

  • Detecting communities in networks: In a network, dense subgraphs often correspond to communities. Parts of the network where nodes are more strongly connected than in the rest of the network tends to signify the presence of a friend group, in social media.
  • Keyword and Entity Extraction: In Natural Language Processing (NLP), graphs can be used to represent co-occurrences between words or entities. Therefore, finding a dense subgraph can be used to identify the most important words/keywords in a large piece of text.
  • Anomaly Detection: In cybersecurity, a sudden appearance of a dense subgraph could be used to trace or indicate a cyber attack.
